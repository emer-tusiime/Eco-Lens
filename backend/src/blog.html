DET-00 · What it is

A machine that turns one returned bottle into something you actually want.

Plastic bottle in. Airtime out. That's the whole idea.

You walk up to the kiosk and drop in an empty plastic bottle. A camera looks at it and decides whether it's really a plastic bottle — not a tin, not random rubbish. If it is, a small gate swings open, the bottle drops into the bin, and you earn points.

Those points aren't just a number on a screen. Through the mobile app you redeem them for mobile airtime, sent straight to your number. The reward is the point: people think twice about tossing a bottle when that same bottle is worth a few shillings of airtime.

Underneath, four pieces work together — a Raspberry Pi kiosk, a machine-learning vision service, a backend that tracks users and points, and a mobile app for cashing out.

DET-01 · The problem

Empty bottles are everywhere — and no one has a reason to deal with them properly.

Plastic bottles are cheap, they're everywhere, and once they're empty most people have no reason to do anything responsible with them. They end up in trenches and drainage channels, worsening flooding in the rains, or burned and buried instead of recycled.

Formal recycling exists, but it's invisible to the ordinary person who just finished a soda. We narrowed it to one honest observation: people respond to incentives. Telling someone recycling is good rarely changes behaviour. Give them airtime — something almost everyone uses and values — and suddenly there's a reason to hold onto that bottle and walk it to a kiosk.

DET-03 · How it works

From bottle to airtime, step by step.

The individual technologies make sense once you see how they fit together.

01

Insert

A user drops an empty bottle into the slot. The kiosk is already on and waiting.

02

See

The IMX219 camera captures the item; the image goes to the YOLOv8 vision service.

03

Check material

An inductive proximity sensor checks for metal — something a camera simply can't tell.

04

Decide & sort

If accepted, a servo gate opens for 3 seconds and the bottle drops into the bin.

05

Points are recorded on the backend, ready to redeem for airtime in the app.

DET-05 · Development progress

Built in stages, on purpose.

Get the software working and trustworthy first, then bring in the hardware and calibrate. That decision saved us.

Phase 1

Backend & database

User registration, login, and the points system, deployed on Railway before any hardware existed. We could create users, record deposits, and watch balances update.

Phase 2

The vision model

Collected and labelled images, trained YOLOv8, and deployed it to Hugging Face Spaces so the backend could send an image and get back a classification.

Phase 3

Mobile app

Built sign-up, points balance, phone linking, and airtime redemption, packaged into an installable Android build via EAS.

Phase 4

Kiosk software

The pygame interface, camera capture, backend calls, and gate logic — with the gate running on a background thread so opening it never freezes the screen.

Phase 5

Hardware integration

Mounting the camera, wiring the servo to its own supply, adding the metal sensor, and auto-booting into the kiosk. Software is fully functional; physical calibration in the final enclosure remains.

DET-06 · Challenges

What broke — the honest version.

Plenty went wrong, and some of it we're still dealing with. The hard parts weren't where we expected.

Solved · core lesson

A camera can't tell plastic from metal

A plastic and a metal bottle of the same shape look identical to a vision model. We added an inductive proximity sensor for a physical material check — the whole sensor-fusion approach came from hitting this wall, not from planning.

Solved

The model called everything a bottle

A model trained only on bottles has no concept of "not a bottle." We stayed strict on the 0.85 confidence threshold so ambiguous objects get rejected rather than waved through.

Solved · hardware

The servo nearly cost us the Pi

The MG996R draws far more current than the Pi can safely supply from GPIO. We gave it its own 5–6V supply and used a GPIO pin only for the control signal.

Solved

The interface kept freezing

Holding the gate open for three seconds froze the whole UI, because everything ran on one thread. Moving the gate to a background thread fixed it.

Solved · airtime is live

Getting airtime to actually send

The reward side now works end to end — points redeem and airtime lands on the user's phone. Getting there meant a silent bug we had to chase: the amount must be sent as a separate numeric value and currency code, never one combined string. We also learned that after changing API credentials you get "authentication invalid" errors for a while purely because the new key takes time to propagate.

Solved · workaround

The touchscreen is sluggish

The cheap resistive panel is noticeably slow to respond to taps. Rather than swap it out this late, we connected a mouse and navigate the kiosk screen with it — far smoother and more reliable for demos than fighting the touch layer.

Solved

The browser blocked our own dashboard

The content security policy blocked our inline scripts and styles, so the admin page loaded blank until we configured the server's security middleware to allow them.

Known limitation

The app doesn't update itself

Some app text is baked in at build time, so backend changes (like the conversion rate) won't show until we rebuild and redistribute the APK.

DET-07 · User manual

How to actually use it.

Split by the three people who touch the system — plus startup for whoever maintains the kiosk.

Depositing a bottle at the kiosk

No buttons to press to begin — the kiosk is always on and waiting.

  1. Walk up to the kiosk. The screen shows a welcome message inviting you to insert a bottle.
  2. Make sure your bottle is empty. Pour out any liquid first — a full bottle is messy and can confuse the sensors.
  3. Insert the bottle into the opening, as shown on screen.
  4. Wait while it's checked. The camera takes a picture and the screen shows it processing. Behind the scenes it checks both the shape and the material.
  5. See the result. Accepted: the screen confirms, the gate opens to drop the bottle in, and you earn points. Rejected (metallic, or not confidently a bottle): the screen says so and the item goes to the reject tray.
  6. Note your points. Accepted deposits add to your account. Use the mobile app to collect and spend them — make sure you're depositing under the right phone number.

Using the mobile app

Where you check your points and cash them out for airtime.

  1. Install the app. Install the EcoLens Android app (the APK) on your phone.
  2. Create an account. Open the app, sign up with your details, and set a password.
  3. Link your phone number. Enter the number you want airtime sent to. This matters — airtime goes here. Use the Ugandan format, +256 followed by your number.
  4. Check your points. The home screen shows your balance, which rises each time the kiosk accepts one of your bottles.
  5. Redeem for airtime. Once you're past the minimum needed to cash out, choose to redeem; the app converts points into a shilling amount.
  6. Confirm and receive. Confirm, and the airtime is sent to your linked number.
Networks: redemptions work for MTN (077, 078, 076, 039) and Airtel (070, 075, 074, 020) Uganda numbers. If your number isn't on one of these, redemption won't go through.

For administrators

Monitoring deposits and the registered kiosk units.

  1. Open the admin dashboard at the /admin page on the deployed backend.
  2. Log in with your administrator account — separate from ordinary user accounts.
  3. Monitor the system. See the kiosk units registered and review the log of disposal sessions and events — what's been deposited and when.
  4. Keep an eye on activity to confirm deposits are being recorded and everything behaves as expected.

Starting up the kiosk

For whoever maintains the machine.

  1. Connect power to the Raspberry Pi and the servo's separate power supply. The servo must never be powered from the Pi itself.
  2. Power on. The Pi boots and automatically launches the kiosk app full-screen via a startup launcher script — no keyboard or mouse needed.
  3. Check the welcome screen appears. Once it shows, the kiosk is ready for deposits.
  4. Confirm internet. The kiosk needs a connection to reach the vision service and backend, so make sure it's online before expecting deposits to work.

Where things stand.

The core system works. Each layer was solid before we stacked the next on top.

A user can deposit a bottle, the machine distinguishes plastic bottles from metal items and random objects, accepted bottles are sorted by the gate, deposits are recorded, points accumulate, and both the app and admin dashboard function.

The sensor-fusion decision is the result we're proudest of — it came from actually hitting the wall with vision-only classification and solving it properly. And the reward loop is now complete: a user redeems points in the app and airtime lands on their phone, which is the whole reason the project exists.

DET-09 · Future improvements

What we'd do next.

Some of it is just finishing what's already in motion.

Calibrate the gate in the final enclosure — the exact servo angles need tuning once everything is mounted. A calibration script is ready.

Train a proper "other" class so the model can confidently say "this is not a bottle" instead of leaning only on a threshold.

Upgrade the touchscreen to something more responsive than the current resistive panel.

Support more networks and regions for airtime, beyond MTN and Airtel Uganda.

Make the app backend-driven so settings like the conversion rate can change without rebuilding the APK.

Add bin-capacity sensing so the system knows when it's full and alerts an admin instead of accepting bottles it can't store.
